Adrenoceptor Antagonist Drugs - Chapter 10
High-Yield Exam-Oriented Review

1. High-Yield Summary
- Adrenoceptor antagonists block effects of catecholamines at alpha or beta receptors.
- Alpha blockers:
  - Nonselective alpha blockers: phenoxybenzamine, phentolamine
  - Alpha-1 blockers: prazosin, terazosin, doxazosin, tamsulosin, alfuzosin, silodosin
  - Alpha-2 blocker: yohimbine
- Beta blockers:
  - Nonselective beta blockers: propranolol, timolol, nadolol
  - Beta-1 selective blockers: metoprolol, atenolol, esmolol, betaxolol, nebivolol
  - Mixed alpha + beta blockers: labetalol, carvedilol
- Major uses:
  - Pheochromocytoma
  - Hypertension
  - Benign prostatic hyperplasia
  - Angina
  - Arrhythmias
  - Heart failure
  - Glaucoma
  - Hyperthyroidism
  - Migraine prophylaxis

2. Core Concepts

Alpha Receptors
- Alpha-1 activation normally causes:
  - Vasoconstriction
  - Increased blood pressure
  - Bladder/prostate smooth muscle contraction
  - Mydriasis
- Alpha-1 blockade causes:
  - Vasodilation
  - Decreased blood pressure
  - Orthostatic hypotension
  - Improved urine flow in BPH

Beta Receptors
- Beta-1 activation normally causes:
  - Increased heart rate
  - Increased contractility
  - Increased renin release
- Beta-1 blockade causes:
  - Decreased heart rate
  - Decreased contractility
  - Decreased renin
  - Decreased myocardial oxygen demand

- Beta-2 activation normally causes:
  - Bronchodilation
  - Vasodilation
  - Glycogenolysis
  - Tremor
- Beta-2 blockade may cause:
  - Bronchoconstriction
  - Worsening asthma/COPD
  - Impaired recovery from hypoglycemia

3. Key Mechanisms / Pathways

Alpha-1 Antagonism
- Blocks Gq-mediated vasoconstriction.
- Decreases IP3/DAG signaling and intracellular calcium effects.
- Results:
  - Arteriolar dilation
  - Venous dilation
  - Reduced peripheral resistance
  - Orthostatic hypotension

Beta Antagonism
- Blocks Gs-mediated cAMP signaling.
- In the heart:
  - Decreased SA node activity
  - Decreased AV conduction
  - Decreased contractility
  - Decreased oxygen demand
- In kidney:
  - Decreased renin release
- In eye:
  - Decreased aqueous humor production

Irreversible Alpha Blockade
- Phenoxybenzamine covalently binds alpha receptors.
- Effect lasts until new receptors are synthesized.
- Useful in pheochromocytoma.

Reversible Alpha Blockade
- Phentolamine and prazosin are competitive/reversible antagonists.
- Blockade can be overcome by high agonist concentrations.

4. Important Drugs

Alpha Antagonists

Phenoxybenzamine
- Nonselective alpha blocker.
- Irreversible.
- Main use: preoperative management of pheochromocytoma.
- Effects:
  - Prevents catecholamine-induced vasoconstriction
  - Controls hypertension before tumor removal
- Adverse effects:
  - Orthostatic hypotension
  - Reflex tachycardia
  - Nasal congestion
  - Inhibition of ejaculation
  - Fatigue/sedation

Phentolamine
- Nonselective alpha blocker.
- Reversible.
- IV use.
- Uses:
  - Pheochromocytoma crisis
  - Catecholamine excess
- Adverse effects:
  - Tachycardia
  - Arrhythmias
  - Myocardial ischemia
  - Orthostatic hypotension

Prazosin / Terazosin / Doxazosin
- Selective alpha-1 blockers.
- Uses:
  - Hypertension
  - Benign prostatic hyperplasia
- Adverse effects:
  - First-dose syncope
  - Orthostatic hypotension
  - Dizziness
  - Reflex tachycardia

Tamsulosin
- Alpha-1A selective antagonist.
- Main use: BPH.
- More prostate-selective, less effect on blood pressure.
- Adverse effects:
  - Orthostatic hypotension
  - Abnormal ejaculation
  - Intraoperative floppy iris syndrome during cataract surgery

Alfuzosin / Silodosin
- Used in BPH.
- Silodosin is alpha-1A selective.
- Alfuzosin may prolong QT in susceptible patients.

Yohimbine
- Alpha-2 antagonist.
- Increases norepinephrine release.
- Can raise blood pressure and heart rate.
- Limited clinical use.

Mixed Alpha + Beta Blockers

Labetalol
- Blocks beta receptors and alpha-1 receptors.
- Uses:
  - Hypertension
  - Hypertensive emergencies
  - Pregnancy-related hypertension
- Less reflex tachycardia than pure alpha blockers.

Carvedilol
- Nonselective beta blocker + alpha-1 blocker.
- Uses:
  - Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
  - Hypertension
- Additional antioxidant/vascular effects may contribute to benefit.

Beta Antagonists

Propranolol
- Nonselective beta blocker.
- Blocks beta-1 and beta-2.
- Uses:
  - Hypertension
  - Angina
  - Arrhythmias
  - Migraine prophylaxis
  - Hyperthyroidism
  - Essential tremor
  - Portal hypertension/variceal bleed prophylaxis
- Adverse effects:
  - Bronchospasm
  - Bradycardia
  - Fatigue
  - Cold extremities
  - Masks hypoglycemia
  - CNS effects due to lipid solubility

Metoprolol / Atenolol
- Beta-1 selective blockers.
- Uses:
  - Hypertension
  - Angina
  - Post-MI mortality reduction
  - Heart failure, especially metoprolol
  - Arrhythmias
- Safer than nonselective beta blockers in asthma/COPD, but still use caution.

Esmolol
- Beta-1 selective.
- Very short acting.
- Half-life about 10 minutes.
- IV only.
- Uses:
  - Acute supraventricular tachycardia
  - Perioperative hypertension
  - Thyrotoxic tachyarrhythmias
  - Acute situations needing rapid titration

Timolol
- Nonselective beta blocker.
- Important use: glaucoma, topical.
- Also reduces post-MI mortality.
- Can still cause systemic beta-blocker effects when used as eye drops.

Nadolol
- Nonselective beta blocker.
- Long duration.
- Used in:
  - Hypertension
  - Migraine prophylaxis
  - Portal hypertension

Nebivolol
- Highly beta-1 selective.
- Promotes nitric oxide-mediated vasodilation.
- Less adverse metabolic effect than older beta blockers.

Pindolol / Acebutolol
- Beta blockers with partial agonist activity.
- May cause less resting bradycardia.
- Not preferred after MI compared with pure antagonists.

Sotalol
- Nonselective beta blocker.
- Also class III antiarrhythmic due to potassium channel blockade.

5. Clinical Correlations

Pheochromocytoma
- Catecholamine-secreting tumor, usually adrenal medulla.
- Symptoms:
  - Episodic hypertension
  - Headache
  - Palpitations
  - Sweating
  - Pallor
- Diagnosis:
  - Elevated plasma or urine metanephrine and normetanephrine
  - Imaging with CT/MRI
  - MIBG scan may localize tumor
- Treatment:
  1. Alpha blockade first: phenoxybenzamine or selective alpha-1 blocker
  2. Volume expansion
  3. Add beta blocker only after alpha blockade if tachycardia persists
  4. Surgical resection
- Never give beta blocker first:
  - Unopposed alpha stimulation can cause severe hypertension.

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia
- Alpha-1A receptors mediate prostate smooth muscle contraction.
- Alpha-1 blockers relax prostate/bladder neck.
- Drugs:
  - Tamsulosin
  - Alfuzosin
  - Silodosin
  - Terazosin
  - Doxazosin
- Clinical benefit:
  - Improved urinary flow
  - Reduced urinary obstruction

Ischemic Heart Disease / Angina
- Beta blockers decrease myocardial oxygen demand by:
  - Decreasing heart rate
  - Decreasing contractility
  - Decreasing blood pressure
- Useful in chronic stable angina.
- Improve survival after MI.

Heart Failure
- Certain beta blockers reduce mortality:
  - Metoprolol
  - Bisoprolol
  - Carvedilol
- Start low and titrate slowly.
- Avoid initiating during acute decompensated heart failure.

Glaucoma
- Topical beta blockers reduce aqueous humor production.
- Drugs:
  - Timolol
  - Betaxolol
  - Levobunolol
  - Carteolol
- Betaxolol is beta-1 selective and may have less bronchospasm risk.

Hyperthyroidism
- Beta blockers reduce adrenergic symptoms:
  - Palpitations
  - Tremor
  - Anxiety
  - Tachycardia
- Propranolol is commonly used in thyroid storm.

Migraine Prophylaxis
- Drugs:
  - Propranolol
  - Metoprolol
  - Timolol
  - Atenolol
  - Nadolol

Portal Hypertension
- Nonselective beta blockers reduce portal pressure.
- Drugs:
  - Propranolol
  - Nadolol
- Used to prevent variceal bleeding.

6. Adverse Effects / Toxicity / Pitfalls

Alpha Blockers
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Reflex tachycardia
- Dizziness/syncope
- Nasal congestion
- Ejaculatory dysfunction
- First-dose syncope, especially prazosin
- Intraoperative floppy iris syndrome with tamsulosin

Beta Blockers
- Bradycardia
- AV block
- Worsening heart failure if started aggressively
- Bronchoconstriction, especially nonselective agents
- Fatigue
- Cold extremities
- Sexual dysfunction
- Depression or sleep disturbance, especially lipophilic agents
- Masks hypoglycemia symptoms
- Delays recovery from hypoglycemia
- Rebound tachycardia/angina/hypertension if abruptly stopped

Important Contraindications / Cautions
- Asthma: avoid nonselective beta blockers.
- Severe bradycardia or AV block: avoid beta blockers.
- Acute decompensated heart failure: do not initiate beta blockers.
- Diabetes with frequent hypoglycemia: use caution.
- BPH with orthostatic hypotension risk: use caution with alpha blockers.
- Cataract surgery: ask about tamsulosin due to floppy iris syndrome.

8. Must-Know Exam Points
- Phenoxybenzamine = irreversible alpha blocker for pheochromocytoma.
- Phentolamine = reversible alpha blocker, IV, catecholamine crisis.
- Prazosin causes first-dose syncope.
- Tamsulosin targets alpha-1A in prostate.
- Tamsulosin is associated with intraoperative floppy iris syndrome.
- Beta blockers decrease renin release.
- Beta blockers decrease myocardial oxygen demand.
- Nonselective beta blockers can worsen asthma.
- Beta blockers mask hypoglycemia symptoms.
- Abrupt beta blocker withdrawal can precipitate angina or MI.
- Esmolol has an ultrashort half-life.
- Timolol is used topically for glaucoma.
- Metoprolol, bisoprolol, carvedilol reduce mortality in HFrEF.
- Never give beta blocker before alpha blockade in pheochromocytoma.
- Epinephrine reversal: alpha blockade converts epinephrine pressor effect into depressor effect due to unopposed beta-2 vasodilation.

9. Case-Based Integration

Case Diagnosis
- Most likely diagnosis: pheochromocytoma.
- Clues:
  - Episodic palpitations
  - Severe headaches
  - Drenching sweats
  - Hypertensive spells
  - Attack triggered by abdominal palpation
  - Facial pallor from intense alpha-mediated vasoconstriction

Why did BP and HR rise during abdominal palpation?
- Physical pressure on the adrenal tumor caused catecholamine release.
- Norepinephrine/epinephrine activated:
  - Alpha-1 receptors: intense vasoconstriction and hypertension
  - Beta-1 receptors: tachycardia and palpitations

Treatment Plan
- Confirm with plasma free metanephrines or urinary fractionated metanephrines.
- Localize tumor with CT/MRI or MIBG.
- Begin alpha blockade before surgery:
  - Phenoxybenzamine is classic.
  - Selective alpha-1 blockers may also be used.
- Expand blood volume.
- Add beta blocker only after adequate alpha blockade if tachycardia persists.
- Definitive treatment: surgical resection.

Emergency Pitfall
- Beta blocker before alpha blockade can worsen hypertension by leaving alpha vasoconstriction unopposed.
