Drug Biotransformation — High-Yield Summary
============================================

1. Core Concept
---------------
Drug biotransformation = chemical modification of drugs/xenobiotics by the body.

Main purpose:
- Convert lipophilic drugs into more polar, water-soluble compounds.
- Facilitate renal or biliary excretion.
- Terminate or modify drug activity.

Important:
- Metabolism usually inactivates drugs.
- Some metabolites are active or toxic.
- Some drugs are prodrugs and require metabolism for activation.

2. Why Biotransformation Is Needed
----------------------------------
Many drugs are:
- Lipophilic
- Poorly ionized at physiologic pH
- Protein-bound
- Easily reabsorbed from renal tubules

Without metabolism, many drugs would have prolonged action.

Examples:
- Thiopental
- Pentobarbital

3. Phase I and Phase II Reactions
---------------------------------

Phase I reactions:
- Introduce or expose functional groups.
- Common groups: -OH, -NH2, -SH
- Often make drug more polar.
- May activate, inactivate, or create toxic metabolites.

Common Phase I reactions:
- Oxidation
- Reduction
- Hydrolysis

Phase II reactions:
- Conjugation reactions.
- Add endogenous molecules to drug or Phase I metabolite.
- Usually produce highly polar, inactive metabolites.

Common conjugates:
- Glucuronic acid
- Sulfate
- Acetate
- Glutathione
- Glycine
- Methyl groups

Important:
- Phase II can occur before Phase I.
- Example: isoniazid acetylation before hydrolysis.

4. Main Sites of Drug Metabolism
--------------------------------
Principal organ:
- Liver

Other sites:
- Intestine
- Lung
- Kidney
- Skin
- Brain
- Plasma
- Gut microbiota

Important concept:
- Oral drugs may undergo first-pass metabolism in intestine and liver.

5. First-Pass Metabolism
------------------------
First-pass effect = metabolism before drug reaches systemic circulation.

Occurs mainly in:
- Intestinal wall
- Liver

Consequences:
- Reduced oral bioavailability
- Need for higher oral dose or alternative route

Examples:
- Morphine
- Lidocaine
- Midazolam
- Cyclosporine
- Chlorpromazine

6. Microsomal Mixed Function Oxidase System
-------------------------------------------
Located mainly in smooth endoplasmic reticulum.

Major components:
- Cytochrome P450 enzymes
- NADPH-cytochrome P450 reductase
- NADPH
- Molecular oxygen

General reaction:
- One oxygen atom enters drug.
- One oxygen atom becomes water.

7. Cytochrome P450 System
-------------------------
Cytochrome P450 enzymes are major Phase I enzymes.

Important human CYP enzymes:
- CYP1A2
- CYP2A6
- CYP2B6
- CYP2C8
- CYP2C9
- CYP2C19
- CYP2D6
- CYP2E1
- CYP3A4
- CYP3A5

Most important:
- CYP3A4 metabolizes more than 50% of many prescription drugs.

8. CYP Induction
----------------
Enzyme induction = increased enzyme expression or reduced degradation.

Effect:
- Increased metabolism
- Reduced drug concentration
- Reduced therapeutic effect
- Increased toxic metabolite formation for some drugs

Common inducers:
- Rifampin
- Phenobarbital
- Carbamazepine
- Phenytoin
- St. John’s wort
- Smoking
- Charcoal-broiled foods
- Cruciferous vegetables
- Chronic alcohol use

Clinical examples:
- Rifampin reduces oral contraceptive effectiveness.
- Smoking increases metabolism of some drugs.
- St. John’s wort reduces levels of many CYP3A4 substrates.

9. CYP Inhibition
-----------------
Enzyme inhibition = decreased metabolism.

Effect:
- Increased drug concentration
- Increased toxicity risk
- Especially important for narrow therapeutic index drugs

Common inhibitors:
- Cimetidine
- Ketoconazole
- Itraconazole
- Erythromycin
- Clarithromycin
- Ritonavir
- Grapefruit juice
- Fluoxetine
- Fluconazole
- Amiodarone

Clinical examples:
- Grapefruit juice inhibits intestinal CYP3A4.
- Macrolides can increase levels of CYP3A4 substrates.
- Allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase and increases mercaptopurine toxicity.

10. Phase II Enzymes
--------------------
Major Phase II pathways:

A. Glucuronidation
- Enzyme: UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT)
- Important for bilirubin, morphine, acetaminophen, diazepam

B. Sulfation
- Enzyme: sulfotransferase
- Important for phenols, alcohols, aromatic amines

C. Acetylation
- Enzyme: N-acetyltransferase (NAT)
- Important for isoniazid, sulfonamides, hydralazine

D. Glutathione conjugation
- Enzyme: glutathione-S-transferase
- Important for detoxifying reactive metabolites

E. Methylation
- Uses S-adenosylmethionine
- Important for catecholamines and thiopurines

11. Toxic Metabolites
---------------------
Drug metabolism can produce toxic reactive intermediates.

Toxicity risk increases when:
- Normal conjugation pathways are saturated.
- Glutathione is depleted.
- CYP activation increases.
- Liver disease is present.

Important example:
- Acetaminophen overdose

12. Acetaminophen Metabolism
----------------------------
At therapeutic doses:
- Mostly glucuronidation and sulfation
- Small amount converted by CYP enzymes to toxic NAPQI
- NAPQI detoxified by glutathione

In overdose:
- Glucuronidation and sulfation become saturated.
- More drug goes through CYP pathway.
- NAPQI increases.
- Glutathione becomes depleted.
- Hepatic injury occurs.

Antidote:
- N-acetylcysteine

Mechanism of antidote:
- Replenishes glutathione
- Detoxifies NAPQI

13. Genetic Polymorphisms
-------------------------
Genetic variation affects drug metabolism.

Important enzymes:
- CYP2D6
- CYP2C19
- CYP2C9
- NAT2
- TPMT
- UGT1A1
- BCHE

14. CYP2D6 Polymorphism
-----------------------
Substrates:
- Codeine
- Tramadol
- Metoprolol
- Nortriptyline
- Tamoxifen
- Hydrocodone

Poor metabolizers:
- Reduced activation of codeine to morphine
- Reduced tamoxifen activation
- Higher risk of toxicity from some parent drugs

Ultrarapid metabolizers:
- Excessive conversion of codeine to morphine
- Risk of respiratory depression

15. CYP2C19 Polymorphism
------------------------
Substrates:
- Clopidogrel
- Omeprazole
- Diazepam
- Citalopram
- Voriconazole
- Mephenytoin

Poor metabolizers:
- Reduced clopidogrel activation
- Increased omeprazole effect
- Increased risk of toxicity from some substrates

Ultrarapid metabolizers:
- Increased clopidogrel activation
- Increased bleeding risk

16. CYP2C9 Polymorphism
-----------------------
Substrates:
- Warfarin
- Phenytoin
- Tolbutamide
- NSAIDs

Poor metabolizers:
- Increased warfarin bleeding risk
- Increased phenytoin toxicity

17. NAT2 Polymorphism
---------------------
Important drug:
- Isoniazid

Slow acetylators:
- Increased risk of peripheral neuropathy
- Increased risk of drug-induced lupus-like syndrome

18. TPMT Polymorphism
---------------------
Important drugs:
- 6-mercaptopurine
- Azathioprine
- Thioguanine

Poor metabolizers:
- Severe myelotoxicity risk

19. UGT1A1 Polymorphism
-----------------------
Associated with:
- Gilbert syndrome
- Increased irinotecan toxicity

20. BCHE Polymorphism
---------------------
Important drug:
- Succinylcholine

Deficiency causes:
- Prolonged apnea
- Prolonged paralysis

21. Gut Microbiota and Drug Metabolism
--------------------------------------
Gut bacteria can metabolize drugs.

Important effects:
- Reduce or activate drugs
- Deconjugate glucuronides
- Contribute to enterohepatic recycling
- Modify toxicity

Examples:
- Digoxin metabolism by gut flora
- Antibiotics can increase digoxin levels
- Bacterial beta-glucuronidase contributes to irinotecan and NSAID GI toxicity

22. Diet and Environment
------------------------
Factors affecting metabolism:

Smoking:
- Induces CYP1A enzymes

Charcoal-broiled foods:
- Induce CYP1A enzymes

Cruciferous vegetables:
- Induce CYP1A enzymes

Grapefruit juice:
- Inhibits intestinal CYP3A4

Alcohol:
- Chronic use induces CYP2E1
- Acute use may inhibit metabolism of some drugs

23. Age and Sex
---------------
Neonates and elderly patients:
- Often have altered metabolism
- Higher risk of toxicity

Sex differences:
- May affect metabolism of some drugs, but less predictable in humans.

24. Disease Effects
-------------------
Liver disease:
- Reduces metabolism of many drugs
- Prolongs half-life
- Increases toxicity risk

Examples:
- Diazepam
- Chlordiazepoxide

Cardiac disease:
- Reduces hepatic blood flow
- Affects high-extraction drugs

Examples of flow-limited drugs:
- Lidocaine
- Morphine
- Propranolol
- Verapamil
- Imipramine

Inflammation and infection:
- Cytokines can reduce CYP expression and activity.

25. Biologics Metabolism
------------------------
Biologics include:
- Monoclonal antibodies
- Cytokines
- Fusion proteins
- Recombinant proteins
- Antibody-drug conjugates

Metabolism:
- Not mainly by CYP enzymes
- Degraded by proteolysis into amino acids
- Often lysosomal degradation
- FcRn recycling prolongs half-life of IgG antibodies

Important:
- Biologics may still affect small-molecule drug metabolism by changing cytokine levels.

26. Drug-Drug Interactions
--------------------------
Major mechanisms:
- CYP induction
- CYP inhibition
- Competition for conjugation pathways
- Altered gut microbiota
- Altered transporters
- Cytokine-mediated CYP changes

Important examples:
- Rifampin decreases warfarin and oral contraceptive levels.
- Grapefruit juice increases felodipine, cyclosporine, some statins.
- Ketoconazole increases CYP3A4 substrate levels.
- Allopurinol increases mercaptopurine toxicity.
- St. John’s wort decreases many CYP3A4 substrate levels.

27. Case Study Summary
----------------------
Patient took overdose of:
- Hydrocodone/acetaminophen
- Carisoprodol
- Duloxetine

Findings:
- Jaundice
- High AST/ALT
- High bilirubin
- Prolonged prothrombin time
- Low glucose
- Reduced renal function
- Detectable acetaminophen level 48 hours later
- Hepatitis C positive

Interpretation:
- Severe acetaminophen toxicity
- Ongoing liver failure
- Mixed drug overdose and HCV likely worsened hepatic risk

Management:
- Give N-acetylcysteine
- Provide IV glucose
- Support renal and hepatic function
- Monitor liver tests, coagulation, glucose, renal function, and acetaminophen level

28. Must-Know Exam Pearls
-------------------------
- Drug metabolism usually makes drugs more water-soluble.
- Phase I = oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis.
- Phase II = conjugation.
- CYP3A4 metabolizes many drugs.
- Enzyme induction decreases drug levels but may increase toxic metabolites.
- Enzyme inhibition increases drug levels and toxicity risk.
- Acetaminophen toxicity is due to NAPQI.
- N-acetylcysteine replenishes glutathione.
- CYP2D6 activates codeine and tamoxifen.
- CYP2C19 activates clopidogrel.
- CYP2C9 metabolizes warfarin and phenytoin.
- NAT2 slow acetylators have more isoniazid toxicity.
- TPMT deficiency increases thiopurine toxicity.
- UGT1A1 variants increase irinotecan toxicity.
- Grapefruit juice inhibits intestinal CYP3A4.
- Rifampin is a strong inducer.
- St. John’s wort induces CYP3A4.
