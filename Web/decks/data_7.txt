Cholinoceptor-Activating & Cholinesterase-Inhibiting Drugs

1. High-Yield Summary
- Cholinomimetics mimic acetylcholine (ACh) by:
  - Directly activating cholinoceptors
  - Indirectly increasing endogenous ACh by inhibiting acetylcholinesterase (AChE)
- Receptor classes:
  - Muscarinic receptors: G protein–coupled receptors
  - Nicotinic receptors: ligand-gated Na+/K+ ion channels
- Major clinical uses:
  - Glaucoma
  - Postoperative ileus / urinary retention
  - Myasthenia gravis
  - Reversal of nondepolarizing neuromuscular blockade
  - Alzheimer disease
  - Antimuscarinic toxicity
  - Smoking cessation
- Major toxic syndrome:
  - Cholinergic excess: SLUDGE/DUMBELS + bronchospasm + bradycardia + miosis
  - Organophosphate poisoning: muscarinic + nicotinic + CNS toxicity

2. Core Concepts
- Muscarinic effects resemble parasympathetic activation.
- Nicotinic effects occur at:
  - Autonomic ganglia
  - Neuromuscular junction
  - CNS
- Cholinesterase inhibitors amplify ACh only where ACh is normally released.
- Quaternary agents generally do not enter the CNS.
- Tertiary/lipid-soluble agents can enter the CNS.

3. Key Mechanisms / Pathways

Muscarinic Receptors
| Receptor | Location | Signaling | High-Yield Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| M1 | CNS, autonomic ganglia | Gq → IP3/DAG ↑ | Neural excitation |
| M2 | Heart | Gi → cAMP ↓, K+ efflux ↑ | Bradycardia, ↓ AV conduction |
| M3 | Glands, smooth muscle, endothelium | Gq → IP3/DAG ↑ | Secretions, smooth muscle contraction, NO-mediated vasodilation |
| M4 | CNS | Gi | CNS modulation |
| M5 | CNS | Gq | CNS modulation |

Nicotinic Receptors
| Receptor | Location | Mechanism | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nm | Skeletal neuromuscular junction | Na+/K+ channel opening | Skeletal muscle contraction |
| Nn | Autonomic ganglia, adrenal medulla, CNS | Na+/K+ channel opening | Ganglionic transmission |

4. Important Drugs / Molecules / Systems

Direct-Acting Muscarinic Agonists
| Drug | Key Action | Clinical Use | Toxicity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bethanechol | Muscarinic agonist, little/no nicotinic effect | Postoperative ileus, urinary retention | Bronchospasm, diarrhea, sweating, salivation |
| Pilocarpine | Muscarinic agonist | Glaucoma, Sjögren syndrome | Sweating, salivation, bronchospasm |
| Cevimeline | M3-selective agonist | Xerostomia in Sjögren syndrome | Muscarinic excess |
| Carbachol | Muscarinic + nicotinic agonist | Ophthalmic use in glaucoma | Cholinergic excess |

Direct-Acting Nicotinic Agonists
| Drug | Key Action | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|
| Nicotine | Nn/Nm agonist | Smoking cessation replacement; toxicity in overdose |
| Varenicline | Partial α4β2 nicotinic agonist | Smoking cessation |

Cholinesterase Inhibitors
| Drug/Class | Duration | CNS Entry | Uses |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Edrophonium | 5–15 min | No | Historically myasthenia diagnosis; short-acting |
| Neostigmine | 0.5–4 h | No | Myasthenia gravis, ileus, urinary retention, reversal of NM blockade |
| Pyridostigmine | 4–6 h | No | Long-term myasthenia gravis |
| Physostigmine | 0.5–2 h | Yes | Severe antimuscarinic toxicity |
| Rivastigmine | 8–10 h | Yes | Alzheimer disease |
| Donepezil | Long | Yes | Alzheimer disease |
| Echothiophate | Very long | Minimal | Obsolete/rare glaucoma use |
| Organophosphates | Very long | Yes | Insecticides, nerve agents; cause poisoning |

5. Clinical Correlations

Glaucoma
- Pilocarpine causes:
  - Miosis
  - Ciliary muscle contraction
  - Increased aqueous humor outflow via canal of Schlemm
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma:
  - Initial drug therapy may include pilocarpine
  - Definitive therapy often requires laser iridotomy
- Chronic glaucoma:
  - Prostaglandins and beta-blockers are more commonly used than cholinomimetics.

Postoperative Ileus / Urinary Retention
- Bethanechol or neostigmine can stimulate GI and bladder smooth muscle.
- Do not use if mechanical obstruction is present.

Myasthenia Gravis
- Autoantibodies against nicotinic ACh receptors at the neuromuscular junction.
- Symptoms:
  - Ptosis
  - Diplopia
  - Dysphagia
  - Dysarthria
  - Weakness worsens with use
- Treatment:
  - Pyridostigmine first-line symptomatic therapy
  - Immunosuppression when needed
  - Thymectomy in selected patients
  - IVIG/plasmapheresis for severe crisis
- Excess cholinesterase inhibitor can cause cholinergic crisis with weakness + muscarinic symptoms.

Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome
- Autoantibodies against presynaptic voltage-gated Ca2+ channels.
- ↓ ACh release → proximal muscle weakness.
- Amifampridine/4-aminopyridine block K+ channels → prolong depolarization → ↑ Ca2+ entry → ↑ ACh release.

Alzheimer Disease
- Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine increase CNS ACh.
- Benefit is modest.
- Donepezil has long half-life and lacks tacrine hepatotoxicity.

Antimuscarinic Toxicity
- Physostigmine can reverse severe central and peripheral antimuscarinic toxicity.
- Use cautiously due to risk of seizures, bradyarrhythmias, and cholinergic toxicity.

6. Adverse Effects / Toxicity / Pitfalls

Muscarinic Excess
- DUMBELS:
  - Diarrhea
  - Urination
  - Miosis
  - Bronchospasm/bronchorrhea/bradycardia
  - Emesis
  - Lacrimation
  - Salivation/sweating
- Major life threats:
  - Bronchospasm
  - Bronchorrhea
  - Bradycardia
  - Respiratory failure

Nicotinic Excess
- Fasciculations
- Muscle cramps
- Weakness
- Paralysis
- Hypertension/tachycardia from sympathetic ganglionic stimulation

CNS Excess
- Agitation
- Confusion
- Seizures
- Coma
- Respiratory arrest

Organophosphate Poisoning
- Irreversible/very long AChE inhibition.
- Found in insecticides and nerve agents.
- Causes muscarinic + nicotinic + CNS toxicity.
- “Aging” makes AChE-organophosphate bond resistant to pralidoxime.
- Treat quickly before aging occurs.

Treatment of Organophosphate Poisoning
1. ABCs: airway, breathing, circulation
2. Decontaminate:
   - Remove clothing
   - Wash skin
   - Protect healthcare workers
3. Atropine:
   - Treats muscarinic toxicity
   - Give until bronchorrhea/bronchospasm/bradycardia improve
4. Pralidoxime (2-PAM):
   - Regenerates AChE before aging
   - Treats nicotinic weakness/fasciculations
5. Benzodiazepines:
   - For seizures/agitation
6. Respiratory support:
   - Often required

High-Yield Pitfalls
- Atropine does not reverse nicotinic paralysis.
- Pralidoxime works best before organophosphate aging.
- Physostigmine crosses the BBB; neostigmine does not.
- Bethanechol is contraindicated in mechanical obstruction.
- Cholinesterase inhibitors can worsen asthma/COPD due to bronchoconstriction.
- Cholinesterase inhibitors can cause bradycardia and AV block.

7. Important Comparisons

Direct vs Indirect Cholinomimetics
| Feature | Direct-Acting Agonists | Indirect-Acting AChE Inhibitors |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Bind cholinoceptors | Inhibit ACh breakdown |
| Effect depends on endogenous ACh? | No | Yes |
| Examples | Bethanechol, pilocarpine, nicotine | Neostigmine, pyridostigmine, physostigmine |
| Major use | Glaucoma, urinary retention, xerostomia | Myasthenia, NM blockade reversal, Alzheimer disease |
| Toxicity | Cholinergic excess | Cholinergic excess, organophosphate poisoning |

Muscarinic vs Nicotinic Effects
| Muscarinic | Nicotinic |
|---|---|
| Miosis | Fasciculations |
| Salivation | Muscle weakness |
| Lacrimation | Paralysis |
| Bronchospasm | Hypertension/tachycardia |
| Bronchorrhea | Ganglionic stimulation |
| Bradycardia | Adrenal medulla stimulation |
| Diarrhea/urination | CNS stimulation at high doses |

Myasthenic Crisis vs Cholinergic Crisis
| Feature | Myasthenic Crisis | Cholinergic Crisis |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Too little ACh signaling | Excess AChE inhibitor |
| Weakness | Yes | Yes |
| Muscarinic symptoms | Usually absent | Present |
| Pupils | Normal | Miosis |
| Secretions | Normal | Increased |
| Treatment | Increase MG therapy, ventilatory support, IVIG/plasmapheresis | Hold AChE inhibitor, atropine, support ventilation |

Neostigmine vs Physostigmine
| Feature | Neostigmine | Physostigmine |
|---|---|---|
| Charge | Quaternary | Tertiary |
| CNS entry | No | Yes |
| Uses | Myasthenia, ileus, urinary retention, NM blockade reversal | Antimuscarinic toxicity |
| Major risk | Muscarinic excess, bradycardia | CNS toxicity, seizures |

Organophosphate vs Carbamate AChE Inhibition
| Feature | Organophosphates | Carbamates |
|---|---|---|
| Bond with AChE | Very stable phosphorylated enzyme | Carbamoylated enzyme |
| Duration | Long | Intermediate |
| Aging | Yes | No clinically important aging |
| Examples | Parathion, malathion, sarin | Neostigmine, pyridostigmine, physostigmine |
| Antidote | Atropine + pralidoxime | Usually atropine/supportive; pralidoxime less central |

8. Must-Know Exam Points
- M2 receptors slow heart rate by ↓ cAMP and ↑ K+ conductance.
- M3 receptors cause gland secretion and smooth muscle contraction.
- M3 receptors on endothelium cause NO-mediated vasodilation.
- Pilocarpine treats glaucoma and xerostomia.
- Bethanechol treats postoperative ileus and urinary retention.
- Neostigmine reverses nondepolarizing neuromuscular blockers.
- Pyridostigmine is preferred for chronic myasthenia gravis.
- Physostigmine treats severe antimuscarinic poisoning because it crosses BBB.
- Donepezil/rivastigmine/galantamine treat Alzheimer disease.
- Organophosphate poisoning presents with DUMBELS + fasciculations + CNS symptoms.
- Atropine treats muscarinic symptoms but not nicotinic weakness.
- Pralidoxime treats nicotinic symptoms if given before aging.
- Nicotine overdose can cause seizures, hypertension, arrhythmias, and respiratory paralysis.
- Varenicline is a partial α4β2 nicotinic agonist for smoking cessation.
- Amifampridine helps Lambert-Eaton by increasing presynaptic ACh release.

9. Case-Based Integration

Case Diagnosis
- JM likely has acute organophosphate poisoning from pesticide exposure.
- Clues:
  - Worked in recently sprayed field
  - Sulfur-like odor
  - Symptoms within hours
  - Lacrimation
  - Blurred vision/miosis
  - Difficulty speaking/swallowing
  - Chest tightness, bronchospasm/bronchorrhea
  - Confusion/agitation
  - Unsteady gait
  - Possible nicotinic weakness

Mechanism
- Organophosphate inhibits acetylcholinesterase.
- ACh accumulates at muscarinic, nicotinic, and CNS synapses.
- Excess ACh causes:
  - Muscarinic: secretions, bronchospasm, bradycardia, diarrhea, miosis
  - Nicotinic: fasciculations, weakness, paralysis
  - CNS: agitation, confusion, seizures, coma

Management
- Remove from exposure.
- Decontaminate:
  - Remove clothing
  - Wash skin thoroughly
  - Isolate contaminated clothing
- Support airway and breathing.
- Give oxygen and ventilatory support if needed.
- Give atropine IV until bronchial secretions and bronchospasm improve.
- Give pralidoxime early to regenerate AChE before aging.
- Give benzodiazepines for seizures/agitation.
- Monitor coworker:
  - Decontaminate if exposed
  - Observe for cholinergic symptoms
  - Treat if symptomatic

Emergency Considerations
- Respiratory failure is the main cause of death.
- Do not delay atropine for lab confirmation.
- Repeated atropine dosing may be required.
- Pralidoxime is time-sensitive.
- Healthcare workers must avoid secondary contamination.

Flashcards

Q: Which muscarinic receptor slows the heart?
A: M2.

Q: Which muscarinic receptor mediates gland secretion and bronchoconstriction?
A: M3.

Q: Why can acetylcholine cause vasodilation despite vascular smooth muscle contraction via M3?
A: Endothelial M3 activation releases nitric oxide, causing smooth muscle relaxation.

Q: Which cholinomimetic treats urinary retention?
A: Bethanechol.

Q: Which cholinomimetic treats Sjögren xerostomia?
A: Pilocarpine or cevimeline.

Q: Which cholinesterase inhibitor is preferred for chronic myasthenia gravis?
A: Pyridostigmine.

Q: Which cholinesterase inhibitor reverses antimuscarinic toxicity?
A: Physostigmine.

Q: Why does physostigmine reverse central antimuscarinic toxicity?
A: It is tertiary/lipid-soluble and crosses the blood-brain barrier.

Q: What is the antidote combination for organophosphate poisoning?
A: Atropine + pralidoxime + supportive care.

Q: What does atropine reverse in organophosphate poisoning?
A: Muscarinic toxicity.

Q: What does pralidoxime reverse?
A: AChE inhibition, especially nicotinic weakness/fasciculations, if given before aging.

Q: What is “aging”?
A: Strengthening of the organophosphate-AChE bond, making reactivation difficult.

Q: What receptor does varenicline partially activate?
A: α4β2 nicotinic receptor.

Q: What is the mechanism of amifampridine?
A: Blocks presynaptic K+ channels, prolonging depolarization and increasing ACh release.

Board-Style MCQs

1. A patient has urinary retention after surgery. Imaging shows no obstruction. Which drug is most appropriate?
A. Atropine
B. Bethanechol
C. Varenicline
D. Donepezil
Answer: B. Bethanechol stimulates muscarinic receptors in the bladder detrusor.

2. A farmer presents with lacrimation, diarrhea, bronchospasm, fasciculations, and confusion after pesticide exposure. Best initial therapy?
A. Flumazenil only
B. Atropine and pralidoxime
C. Naloxone
D. Physostigmine
Answer: B. This is organophosphate poisoning.

3. A child ingests atropine and develops fever, delirium, mydriasis, and tachycardia. Which antidote crosses the BBB?
A. Neostigmine
B. Pyridostigmine
C. Physostigmine
D. Bethanechol
Answer: C. Physostigmine is tertiary and enters the CNS.

4. A patient with myasthenia gravis is treated chronically with which agent?
A. Pyridostigmine
B. Pilocarpine
C. Varenicline
D. Nicotine
Answer: A. Pyridostigmine is commonly used for chronic symptomatic MG treatment.

5. A patient with Lambert-Eaton syndrome improves with amifampridine. What is the mechanism?
A. Blocks ACh breakdown
B. Blocks presynaptic K+ channels
C. Directly activates Nm receptors
D. Blocks muscarinic receptors
Answer: B. It prolongs presynaptic depolarization and increases ACh release.
