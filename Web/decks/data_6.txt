Introduction to Autonomic Pharmacology — High-Yield Summary
============================================================

1. Core Concept
---------------
Autonomic pharmacology studies drugs that affect the autonomic nervous system (ANS).

The ANS controls involuntary visceral functions such as:
- Heart rate
- Blood pressure
- Digestion
- Bronchial tone
- Pupil size
- Gland secretion
- Bladder function
- Sexual function

The ANS works mainly through chemical neurotransmission.

2. Nervous System Divisions
---------------------------
Anatomic division:
- Central nervous system (CNS): brain and spinal cord
- Peripheral nervous system (PNS): neural tissue outside CNS

Functional division:
- Autonomic nervous system
- Somatic nervous system

Autonomic system:
- Mostly involuntary
- Controls visceral organs

Somatic motor system:
- Mostly voluntary
- Controls skeletal muscle

3. Major Divisions of the ANS
-----------------------------
The ANS has two major anatomic divisions:

A. Sympathetic nervous system
- Thoracolumbar outflow
- Preganglionic fibers arise from thoracic and lumbar spinal cord
- Associated with “fight or flight”

B. Parasympathetic nervous system
- Cranial outflow, traditionally craniosacral
- Preganglionic fibers arise from brainstem cranial nerves
- Associated with “rest and digest”

Important update:
- Sacral autonomic outflow may be sympathetic rather than parasympathetic based on developmental evidence.

4. Basic Autonomic Pathway
--------------------------
Autonomic efferent pathway usually has two neurons:

1. Preganglionic neuron
- Cell body in CNS
- Axon exits CNS
- Synapses in autonomic ganglion

2. Postganglionic neuron
- Cell body in peripheral ganglion
- Axon goes to effector organ

Exception:
- Adrenal medulla acts like a modified sympathetic ganglion.

5. Sympathetic Anatomy
----------------------
Sympathetic preganglionic fibers:
- Usually short
- Synapse in paravertebral or prevertebral ganglia

Sympathetic postganglionic fibers:
- Usually long
- Innervate target organs

Adrenal medulla:
- Receives sympathetic preganglionic fibers
- Releases epinephrine and norepinephrine into blood

6. Parasympathetic Anatomy
--------------------------
Parasympathetic preganglionic fibers:
- Usually long
- Travel through cranial nerves III, VII, IX, and X

Parasympathetic ganglia:
- Often located near or inside target organs

Parasympathetic postganglionic fibers:
- Usually short

7. Enteric Nervous System
-------------------------
The enteric nervous system (ENS) is a large network of neurons in the GI tract.

Main plexuses:
- Myenteric plexus / Auerbach plexus
- Submucosal plexus / Meissner plexus

Functions:
- Controls GI motility
- Controls GI secretion
- Coordinates peristalsis
- Communicates with sympathetic and parasympathetic systems

Important:
- ENS can function semi-autonomously.
- It receives modulatory input from the ANS.

8. Neurotransmitters in the ANS
-------------------------------
Main autonomic neurotransmitters:
- Acetylcholine
- Norepinephrine

Other transmitters/cotransmitters:
- Dopamine
- ATP
- Nitric oxide
- Neuropeptide Y
- Substance P
- Vasoactive intestinal peptide
- Calcitonin gene-related peptide
- Serotonin
- GABA
- Enkephalins

9. Cholinergic Fibers
---------------------
Cholinergic fibers release acetylcholine.

Cholinergic neurons include:
- All autonomic preganglionic neurons
- Most parasympathetic postganglionic neurons
- Some sympathetic postganglionic neurons
- Somatic motor neurons to skeletal muscle

Examples of sympathetic cholinergic fibers:
- Sweat gland innervation
- Some skeletal muscle vasodilator fibers

10. Adrenergic Fibers
---------------------
Adrenergic fibers release norepinephrine.

Most sympathetic postganglionic fibers are noradrenergic.

Adrenal medulla releases:
- Epinephrine
- Norepinephrine

11. Cholinergic Transmission
----------------------------
Acetylcholine synthesis:
- Choline + acetyl-CoA → acetylcholine
- Enzyme: choline acetyltransferase

Choline uptake:
- Via sodium-dependent choline transporter
- Blocked by hemicholinium

Storage:
- Acetylcholine stored in vesicles
- Vesicular acetylcholine transporter loads ACh into vesicles
- Blocked by vesamicol

Release:
- Action potential opens voltage-gated calcium channels
- Calcium triggers vesicle fusion
- ACh released into synaptic cleft

Termination:
- Acetylcholinesterase rapidly breaks down ACh
- Products: acetate + choline

12. Botulinum Toxin
-------------------
Botulinum toxin blocks acetylcholine release.

Mechanism:
- Cleaves SNARE fusion proteins
- Prevents vesicle fusion with nerve terminal membrane
- Blocks exocytotic ACh release

Clinical effect:
- Muscle paralysis or relaxation

Uses:
- Blepharospasm
- Dystonia
- Spasticity
- Cosmetic reduction of wrinkles
- Hyperhidrosis
- Some migraine protocols

Duration:
- Usually 1–3 months after one treatment

Case relevance:
- Botulinum toxin improved vision by relaxing orbicularis oculi muscle and preventing involuntary eyelid closure.

13. Tetanus Toxin
-----------------
Tetanus toxin also cleaves fusion proteins.

Major site:
- Inhibitory neurons in spinal cord

Effect:
- Blocks inhibitory transmitter release
- Causes muscle spasm rather than paralysis

14. Adrenergic Transmission
---------------------------
Catecholamine synthesis pathway:
- Tyrosine → DOPA
- DOPA → dopamine
- Dopamine → norepinephrine
- Norepinephrine → epinephrine

Key enzymes:
- Tyrosine hydroxylase
- DOPA decarboxylase
- Dopamine beta-hydroxylase
- Phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase

Rate-limiting step:
- Tyrosine → DOPA
- Enzyme: tyrosine hydroxylase

Inhibitor:
- Metyrosine

15. Adrenergic Storage
----------------------
Norepinephrine is stored in vesicles.

Transporter:
- Vesicular monoamine transporter (VMAT)

VMAT inhibitors:
- Reserpine
- Tetrabenazine
- Deutetrabenazine

Effect:
- Deplete catecholamine stores

16. Adrenergic Release
----------------------
Physiologic release:
- Action potential opens calcium channels
- Calcium triggers vesicle fusion
- Norepinephrine released

Release inhibitors:
- Guanethidine
- Bretylium

Indirect sympathomimetics:
- Tyramine
- Amphetamine
- Ephedrine

Mechanism:
- Enter nerve terminal via norepinephrine transporter
- Displace norepinephrine from vesicles
- Promote norepinephrine release by reverse transport

17. Termination of Adrenergic Action
------------------------------------
Main mechanisms:
- Reuptake into nerve terminal by NET
- Diffusion away from receptor site
- Metabolism by MAO and COMT

NET inhibitors:
- Cocaine
- Tricyclic antidepressants
- SNRIs

Effect of NET inhibition:
- Increased norepinephrine in synaptic cleft
- Increased adrenergic effect

18. Catecholamine Metabolism
----------------------------
Important enzymes:
- MAO = monoamine oxidase
- COMT = catechol-O-methyltransferase

Major metabolite:
- VMA = vanillylmandelic acid

Clinical relevance:
- Urinary catecholamine metabolites can help assess catecholamine turnover.

19. Autonomic Receptors
-----------------------
Major receptor families:
- Cholinoceptors
- Adrenoceptors
- Dopamine receptors

20. Cholinoceptors
------------------
Two main types:
- Muscarinic receptors
- Nicotinic receptors

21. Muscarinic Receptors
------------------------
Muscarinic receptors respond to acetylcholine and muscarine.

Subtypes:
- M1
- M2
- M3
- M4
- M5

M1:
- CNS neurons
- Sympathetic ganglia
- Increases IP3/DAG and intracellular calcium

M2:
- Heart
- Some smooth muscle
- Opens potassium channels
- Inhibits adenylyl cyclase
- Slows heart rate

M3:
- Exocrine glands
- Smooth muscle
- Vascular endothelium
- Increases IP3/DAG and intracellular calcium

M4:
- CNS
- Similar to M2

M5:
- CNS
- Cerebral vascular endothelium
- Similar to M1

22. Nicotinic Receptors
-----------------------
Nicotinic receptors are ligand-gated ion channels.

Types:
- NN
- NM

NN receptors:
- Autonomic ganglia
- Adrenal medulla

NM receptors:
- Skeletal muscle neuromuscular junction

Activation:
- Opens Na+ and K+ channels
- Causes depolarization

23. Adrenoceptors
-----------------
Adrenoceptors respond to norepinephrine and epinephrine.

Major types:
- Alpha receptors
- Beta receptors

24. Alpha Receptors
-------------------
Alpha1:
- Mostly postsynaptic
- Smooth muscle contraction
- Uses IP3/DAG pathway

Common effects:
- Vasoconstriction
- Pupillary dilation
- Sphincter contraction

Alpha2:
- Often presynaptic
- Inhibits norepinephrine release
- Inhibits adenylyl cyclase
- Decreases cAMP

Common effects:
- Negative feedback on sympathetic nerve terminal
- Platelet aggregation
- Some vascular smooth muscle contraction

25. Beta Receptors
------------------
Beta1:
- Heart
- Kidney juxtaglomerular cells

Effects:
- Increased heart rate
- Increased contractility
- Increased conduction
- Increased renin release

Beta2:
- Bronchial smooth muscle
- Vascular smooth muscle in skeletal muscle
- Uterine smooth muscle
- Liver

Effects:
- Bronchodilation
- Vasodilation
- Uterine relaxation
- Glycogenolysis
- Gluconeogenesis

Beta3:
- Adipose tissue
- Bladder detrusor muscle

Effects:
- Lipolysis
- Bladder relaxation

26. Dopamine Receptors
----------------------
D1:
- Renal vascular smooth muscle
- Causes vasodilation
- Increases cAMP

D2:
- Brain
- Presynaptic nerve terminals
- Inhibits adenylyl cyclase
- Reduces transmitter release

D3, D4, D5:
- Mostly CNS roles

27. Nonadrenergic, Noncholinergic Neurons
-----------------------------------------
NANC neurons do not use norepinephrine or acetylcholine as their main transmitter.

Transmitters include:
- Nitric oxide
- ATP
- VIP
- Substance P
- CGRP
- Neuropeptide Y
- Serotonin

Important locations:
- GI tract
- Airways
- Bladder
- Blood vessels

28. Nitric Oxide
----------------
Nitric oxide is a major NANC transmitter.

Features:
- Not stored in vesicles
- Synthesized on demand by nitric oxide synthase
- Diffuses locally
- Causes smooth muscle relaxation

Important role:
- Vascular relaxation
- GI sphincter relaxation
- Penile erection

29. Autonomic Reflexes
----------------------
The ANS uses reflexes to maintain homeostasis.

Important cardiovascular reflex:
- Baroreceptor reflex

Sensors:
- Carotid sinus
- Aortic arch

Controlled variable:
- Mean arterial pressure

If blood pressure rises:
- Baroreceptor firing increases
- Sympathetic outflow decreases
- Parasympathetic outflow increases
- Heart rate decreases
- Vascular tone decreases

If blood pressure falls:
- Baroreceptor firing decreases
- Sympathetic outflow increases
- Parasympathetic outflow decreases
- Heart rate increases
- Contractility increases
- Vasoconstriction occurs
- Renin release increases

30. Example: Norepinephrine Infusion
------------------------------------
Direct effects:
- Vasoconstriction
- Increased blood pressure
- Direct cardiac stimulation

Reflex effect:
- Increased blood pressure activates baroreceptors
- Increased vagal output slows heart rate

Net effect:
- Increased blood pressure
- Often reflex bradycardia

31. Presynaptic Regulation
--------------------------
Presynaptic receptors modulate transmitter release.

Autoreceptors:
- Respond to the same transmitter released by the neuron

Heteroreceptors:
- Respond to transmitters released by nearby neurons or tissues

Example:
- Alpha2 autoreceptors on adrenergic terminals inhibit norepinephrine release.

Other presynaptic modulators:
- Acetylcholine
- Dopamine
- Serotonin
- ATP
- Adenosine
- Histamine
- Enkephalins
- Neuropeptide Y
- Prostaglandins

32. Postsynaptic Regulation
---------------------------
Postsynaptic responses can be modified by:
- Receptor upregulation
- Receptor downregulation
- Desensitization
- Interactions with other receptors

Denervation supersensitivity:
- Loss of nerve input causes increased receptor sensitivity.
- Example: increased nicotinic receptors after skeletal muscle denervation.

33. Ganglionic Transmission
---------------------------
Autonomic ganglia use nicotinic receptors for fast transmission.

Main event:
- ACh activates NN receptors
- Fast excitatory postsynaptic potential occurs

Additional slower effects:
- Muscarinic receptors can modify ganglionic excitability
- Peptides can cause slow postsynaptic potentials

34. Eye Pharmacology
--------------------
Important autonomic tissues in the eye:
- Iris radial muscle
- Iris circular muscle
- Ciliary muscle
- Ciliary epithelium

Iris radial muscle:
- Receptor: alpha1
- Effect: contraction
- Result: mydriasis

Iris circular muscle:
- Receptor: M3
- Effect: contraction
- Result: miosis

Ciliary muscle:
- Receptor: M3
- Effect: contraction
- Result: accommodation for near vision
- Also opens trabecular meshwork and increases aqueous outflow

Ciliary epithelium:
- Receptor: beta
- Effect: aqueous humor secretion

35. Glaucoma Pharmacology Concept
---------------------------------
Increased aqueous humor outflow:
- Muscarinic agonists contract ciliary muscle
- Opens trabecular meshwork
- Reduces intraocular pressure

Decreased aqueous humor production:
- Beta blockers reduce secretion by ciliary epithelium
- Reduces intraocular pressure

36. Major Direct Effects of Sympathetic Activity
------------------------------------------------
Eye:
- Iris radial muscle contracts → mydriasis

Heart:
- Increased heart rate
- Increased contractility
- Increased conduction

Blood vessels:
- Alpha1 vasoconstriction in skin and splanchnic vessels
- Beta2 vasodilation in skeletal muscle vessels

Bronchi:
- Beta2 bronchodilation

GI tract:
- Reduced motility
- Sphincter contraction

Bladder:
- Detrusor relaxation
- Sphincter contraction

Metabolic:
- Glycogenolysis
- Gluconeogenesis
- Lipolysis
- Renin release

37. Major Direct Effects of Parasympathetic Activity
----------------------------------------------------
Eye:
- Miosis
- Accommodation for near vision

Heart:
- Decreased heart rate
- Decreased atrial contractility

Bronchi:
- Bronchoconstriction
- Increased secretion

GI tract:
- Increased motility
- Increased secretion
- Sphincter relaxation

Bladder:
- Detrusor contraction
- Sphincter relaxation

Sexual function:
- Erection

38. Pharmacologic Targets in Autonomic Transmission
---------------------------------------------------
Possible drug targets:
- Action potential propagation
- Transmitter synthesis
- Transmitter storage
- Transmitter release
- Transmitter reuptake
- Receptor activation
- Receptor blockade
- Transmitter degradation

Examples:
- Local anesthetics block sodium channels.
- Hemicholinium blocks choline uptake.
- Vesamicol blocks ACh vesicular storage.
- Botulinum toxin blocks ACh release.
- Metyrosine blocks catecholamine synthesis.
- Reserpine blocks catecholamine storage.
- Cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake.
- Neostigmine inhibits acetylcholinesterase.
- Atropine blocks muscarinic receptors.
- Propranolol blocks beta receptors.
- Phentolamine blocks alpha receptors.

39. Case Study Summary
----------------------
Patient:
- 56-year-old woman
- Involuntary eyelid closure
- Diagnosed with blepharospasm
- Treated with botulinum toxin type A injections into orbicularis oculi

Mechanism:
- Botulinum toxin blocks acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junction.
- Prevents skeletal muscle contraction.
- Relaxes orbicularis oculi muscle.
- Eyelids no longer close involuntarily.

Expected duration:
- About 1–3 months after a single treatment.

40. Must-Know Exam Pearls
-------------------------
- All autonomic preganglionic neurons release acetylcholine.
- Most parasympathetic postganglionic neurons release acetylcholine.
- Most sympathetic postganglionic neurons release norepinephrine.
- Sweat glands receive sympathetic cholinergic innervation.
- Adrenal medulla releases epinephrine and norepinephrine into blood.
- Nicotinic NN receptors are in autonomic ganglia.
- Nicotinic NM receptors are at skeletal neuromuscular junction.
- M2 receptors slow the heart.
- M3 receptors contract smooth muscle and stimulate glands.
- Alpha1 causes vasoconstriction and mydriasis.
- Alpha2 inhibits norepinephrine release.
- Beta1 increases heart rate, contractility, and renin release.
- Beta2 causes bronchodilation and vasodilation.
- Beta3 causes lipolysis and bladder relaxation.
- Botulinum toxin blocks ACh release by cleaving SNARE proteins.
- Acetylcholinesterase terminates ACh action.
- NET reuptake terminates much norepinephrine action.
- MAO and COMT metabolize catecholamines.
- Baroreceptor reflex can reverse direct cardiac effects of adrenergic drugs.
- Norepinephrine can cause reflex bradycardia despite direct beta1 stimulation.
