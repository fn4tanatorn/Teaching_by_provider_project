# Pharmacokinetics & Pharmacodynamics: Rational Dosing & Time Course of Drug Action — High-Yield Summary

## 1. Core Concept

Drug response depends on:

**Dose → Concentration → Effect**

- **Pharmacokinetics (PK)** = what the body does to the drug
  - Absorption
  - Distribution
  - Metabolism
  - Excretion

- **Pharmacodynamics (PD)** = what the drug does to the body
  - Drug concentration–effect relationship

---

## 2. Main Pharmacokinetic Parameters

### A. Volume of Distribution (Vd)

Formula:

```text
Vd = Amount of drug in body / Plasma concentration
```

Meaning:

- Apparent space containing the drug
- Large Vd → drug mainly in tissues
- Small Vd → drug remains mostly in plasma

Examples:

- **Digoxin** → very large Vd
- **Gentamicin** → low Vd

Typical compartments:

| Compartment | Approximate Volume |
|---|---:|
| Plasma | 0.04 L/kg |
| Extracellular fluid | 0.2 L/kg |
| Total body water | 0.6 L/kg |

---

### B. Clearance (CL)

Formula:

```text
CL = Rate of elimination / Drug concentration
```

Meaning:

- Efficiency of drug elimination

Major elimination organs:

- Kidney
- Liver

Total clearance:

```text
CLtotal = CLrenal + CLhepatic + CLother
```

---

### C. Half-Life (t1/2)

Formula:

```text
t1/2 = 0.7 × Vd / CL
```

Meaning:

- Time required for drug amount to decrease by 50%

Rules:

- 1 half-life → 50%
- 2 half-lives → 75%
- 4–5 half-lives → about 95% steady state

---

## 3. Types of Elimination

### First-Order Elimination

- Most drugs
- Constant fraction removed per unit time

Examples:

- Digoxin
- Theophylline

---

### Capacity-Limited / Nonlinear Elimination

Elimination pathways become saturated.

Examples:

- Phenytoin
- Ethanol
- High-dose aspirin

Michaelis-Menten relationship:

```text
Rate = (Vmax × C) / (Km + C)
```

Features:

- Small dose increase may cause large concentration increase
- Higher toxicity risk

---

### Flow-Dependent Elimination

Elimination depends mainly on organ blood flow.

Examples:

- Morphine
- Lidocaine
- Propranolol

---

## 4. Drug Accumulation

Repeated dosing causes accumulation until steady state.

Depends on:

- Dosing interval
- Half-life

Rule:

- Short dosing interval relative to half-life → more accumulation

---

## 5. Bioavailability (F)

Definition:

- Fraction of unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation

IV drugs:

```text
F = 1
```

Oral drugs:

```text
F < 1
```

Reasons for low bioavailability:

1. Poor absorption
2. First-pass metabolism

Examples:

- Morphine → extensive first-pass effect
- Propranolol → high first-pass metabolism

---

## 6. First-Pass Effect

Drug absorbed from the GI tract enters portal circulation, then passes through the liver before reaching systemic circulation.

Consequences:

- Reduced bioavailability
- Increased metabolite formation

Routes that avoid or reduce first-pass effect:

- Sublingual
- Transdermal
- Partial rectal route

---

## 7. Routes of Administration

| Route | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| IV | 100% bioavailability |
| Oral | Convenient, first-pass effect possible |
| IM / SC | Good absorption |
| Inhalation | Rapid onset |
| Transdermal | Slow prolonged release |

---

## 8. Loading Dose

Used to rapidly achieve target concentration.

Formula:

```text
Loading dose = Vd × Target concentration
```

Purpose:

- Useful for drugs with long half-life

Examples:

- Digoxin
- Phenytoin

---

## 9. Maintenance Dose

Used to maintain steady-state concentration.

Formula:

```text
Dosing rate = CL × Target concentration
```

Oral dosing:

```text
Dose = (CL × Target concentration × Dosing interval) / F
```

Main determinant:

- **Clearance**

---

## 10. Concentration–Effect Relationship

Drug effect often follows the **Emax model**.

Key terms:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Emax | Maximal effect |
| C50 / EC50 | Concentration producing 50% effect |

Important:

- Concentration changes do not always produce proportional effect changes.

---

## 11. Delayed Drug Effects

Causes:

1. Distribution delay
2. Slow receptor dissociation
3. Slow physiologic turnover

Examples:

- Digoxin
- Warfarin

Warfarin:

- Rapidly inhibits VKOR
- INR effect is delayed because clotting factors have long half-lives

---

## 12. Schedule-Dependent Toxicity

Toxicity may depend on dosing pattern.

Example:

- Aminoglycosides

Continuous infusion:

- More nephrotoxicity

Intermittent dosing:

- Less renal uptake

---

## 13. Cumulative Drug Effects

Effect depends on total exposure.

Important measure:

```text
AUC = Area under the concentration-time curve
```

Examples:

- Many chemotherapy drugs

---

## 14. Target Concentration Strategy

Goal:

- Achieve desired therapeutic concentration

Steps:

1. Choose target concentration
2. Estimate Vd and CL
3. Calculate dose
4. Measure response or concentration
5. Adjust regimen

---

## 15. Factors Affecting Drug Levels

### A. Renal Function

Reduced renal clearance affects drugs such as:

- Digoxin
- Aminoglycosides

Use creatinine clearance for dose adjustment.

---

### B. Hepatic Disease

May reduce metabolism.

Examples:

- Diazepam
- Propranolol

---

### C. Obesity

Affects Vd differently:

- Lipophilic drugs → larger Vd
- Hydrophilic drugs → minimal change

---

### D. Edema / Ascites

May increase Vd of hydrophilic drugs.

Example:

- Gentamicin

---

## 16. Plasma Protein Binding

Important mainly for interpretation of total drug concentration.

Key proteins:

- Albumin
- Alpha-1 acid glycoprotein

Important point:

- Changes in protein binding usually affect total drug concentration more than free drug effect.

---

## 17. Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)

Useful for drugs with:

- Narrow therapeutic index
- Variable pharmacokinetics

Examples:

- Digoxin
- Lithium
- Aminoglycosides
- Phenytoin

Timing matters:

| Drug | Sampling Note |
|---|---|
| Digoxin | Measure after distribution, usually at least 6 h after dose |
| Lithium | Trough level |
| Aminoglycosides | Peak/trough monitoring |

---

## 18. Digoxin Case Summary

Patient:

- Elderly
- Reduced renal function

Key principle:

- Digoxin clearance is mainly renal.

Approach:

1. Estimate creatinine clearance
2. Calculate total clearance
3. Calculate maintenance dose

Recommended practical dose:

```text
125 mcg/day = two 62.5 mcg tablets daily
```

---

## Must-Know Exam Pearls

- Clearance determines maintenance dose.
- Volume of distribution determines loading dose.
- Half-life depends on both Vd and CL.
- Steady state is reached after about 4–5 half-lives.
- First-pass metabolism lowers oral bioavailability.
- Nonlinear elimination: phenytoin, ethanol, aspirin.
- Digoxin dose must be reduced in renal failure.
- Aminoglycoside toxicity depends on dosing schedule.
- Protein binding changes usually alter total concentration more than free concentration.
