Drug Receptors & Pharmacodynamics — High-Yield Summary

1.  Drug Receptors

-   Receptors are usually proteins that bind drugs and initiate biologic responses.
-   Main types:
    -   Regulatory proteins
    -   Enzymes
    -   Ion channels
    -   Transport proteins

2.  Major Principles

-   Receptors determine dose–response relationships.
-   Receptors determine selectivity.
-   Agonists activate receptors.
-   Antagonists bind without activation.

Key terms:
- Kd = concentration causing 50% receptor occupancy
- EC50 = concentration causing 50% maximal effect
- Emax = maximal response

3.  Dose–Response Curves

-   Increasing dose increases response until plateau.
-   Log-dose plots create sigmoid curves for easier interpretation.

4.  Spare Receptors

-   Maximal response can occur before all receptors are occupied.
-   Increases tissue sensitivity.

5.  Competitive vs Noncompetitive Antagonists

Competitive antagonist:
- Reversible
- Shifts dose-response curve to the right
- Emax unchanged

Noncompetitive antagonist: - Irreversible or allosteric - Decreases Emax

6.  Partial Agonists

-   Produce less than maximal response.
-   Can behave as agonists or antagonists.

Example: - Buprenorphine

7.  Other Antagonism Types

-   Chemical antagonism: protamine vs heparin
-   Physiologic antagonism: insulin vs glucocorticoids

8.  Signal Transduction Mechanisms

A. Intracellular receptors - Steroids, thyroid hormone - Alter gene transcription

B. Receptor tyrosine kinases - Insulin receptor - EGF receptor

C. Cytokine receptors - JAK-STAT pathway

D. Ligand-gated ion channels - Nicotinic ACh receptor - GABA receptor

E. GPCRs - Largest receptor family

Major G proteins: - Gs → ↑ cAMP - Gi → ↓ cAMP - Gq → ↑ IP3/DAG/Ca2+

9.  Second Messengers

cAMP pathway: - Activates protein kinase A

IP3/DAG pathway: - IP3 increases intracellular calcium - DAG activates
PKC

cGMP pathway: - Smooth muscle relaxation - Nitric oxide signaling

10. Receptor Regulation

-   Desensitization
-   Downregulation
-   Upregulation

11. Potency vs Efficacy

-   Potency = amount required
-   Efficacy = maximal effect achievable

12. Quantal Dose–Response Curves

-   ED50 = effective dose in 50%
-   TD50 = toxic dose in 50%
-   LD50 = lethal dose in 50%

Therapeutic index: TI = TD50 / ED50

13. Causes of Variable Drug Response

-   Pharmacokinetics
-   Endogenous ligand levels
-   Receptor changes
-   Post-receptor changes

14. Clinical Selectivity

-   Drugs are selective, not specific.

Case Study:
- Propranolol worsens asthma by blocking β2 receptors.
- Verapamil lowers blood pressure without bronchoconstriction.
- Metoprolol is a safer β1-selective alternative.
