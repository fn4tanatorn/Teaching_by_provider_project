Cholinoceptor-Blocking Drugs
Todd W. Vanderah, PhD
Chapter 8 High-Yield Output

1. High-Yield Summary

- Cholinoceptor antagonists are divided into:
  - Muscarinic receptor blockers = antimuscarinic drugs
  - Nicotinic receptor blockers = ganglion blockers and neuromuscular blockers
- This chapter focuses mainly on antimuscarinic drugs.
- Prototype drug: atropine.
- Antimuscarinic drugs block parasympathetic effects at muscarinic receptors.
- Major clinical uses:
  - Motion sickness: scopolamine
  - Ophthalmology: atropine, homatropine, cyclopentolate, tropicamide
  - COPD/asthma: ipratropium, tiotropium, aclidinium, umeclidinium
  - Overactive bladder/urge incontinence: oxybutynin, tolterodine, darifenacin, solifenacin, fesoterodine, trospium
  - Cholinesterase inhibitor poisoning: atropine + pralidoxime
  - Parkinson tremor: benztropine, trihexyphenidyl
- Classic antimuscarinic toxicity:
  - Dry as a bone
  - Blind as a bat
  - Red as a beet
  - Mad as a hatter
  - Hot as a hare

2. Core Concepts

Muscarinic receptor subtypes:
- M1:
  - Location: CNS, autonomic ganglia, presynaptic nerves
  - Signaling: Gq -> IP3/DAG
  - Drugs: pirenzepine, telenzepine, dicyclomine, trihexyphenidyl
- M2:
  - Location: heart, nerves, smooth muscle
  - Signaling: Gi -> decreased cAMP, increased K+ conductance
  - Effect: slows SA and AV node activity
- M3:
  - Location: glands, smooth muscle, endothelium
  - Signaling: Gq -> IP3/DAG
  - Major exam receptor for secretions, bronchoconstriction, bladder contraction, GI motility, miosis
  - Drugs: oxybutynin, tolterodine, darifenacin, solifenacin, tiotropium
- M4/M5:
  - More important in CNS
  - M5 may have role in drug addiction

Key principle:
- Antimuscarinic drugs block muscarinic receptors but do not significantly block nicotinic receptors at therapeutic doses.

3. Key Mechanisms / Pathways

Atropine mechanism:
- Competitive, reversible muscarinic antagonist
- Blocks acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors
- Prevents:
  - IP3/DAG signaling through M1/M3/M5
  - Gi-mediated effects through M2/M4

M2 cardiac blockade:
- Normally acetylcholine activates M2 receptors -> decreased cAMP + increased K+ efflux
- This slows SA node firing and AV conduction.
- Atropine blocks M2 -> tachycardia and increased AV conduction.

M3 smooth muscle/gland blockade:
- Normally acetylcholine activates M3 -> IP3/DAG -> increased intracellular Ca2+
- Causes gland secretion and smooth muscle contraction.
- Antimuscarinics block this -> dry mouth, bronchodilation, reduced bladder contraction, constipation.

M3 eye blockade:
- Blocks iris sphincter contraction -> mydriasis
- Blocks ciliary muscle contraction -> cycloplegia
- Can precipitate acute angle-closure glaucoma.

4. Important Drugs / Systems

A. Natural alkaloids
- Atropine:
  - Prototype antimuscarinic
  - Tertiary amine
  - Enters CNS
  - Uses: bradycardia, organophosphate poisoning, ophthalmology
- Scopolamine:
  - More CNS effect than atropine
  - Causes sedation and amnesia
  - Used for motion sickness and postoperative nausea/vomiting

B. Ophthalmic antimuscarinics
- Atropine:
  - Duration: 5-6 days
  - Longest acting
- Scopolamine:
  - Duration: 3-7 days
- Homatropine:
  - Duration: 12-24 hours
- Cyclopentolate:
  - Duration: 3-6 hours
- Tropicamide:
  - Duration: 15-60 minutes
  - Best for short funduscopic exam

C. Respiratory antimuscarinics
- Ipratropium:
  - Shorter acting
  - Used in COPD and asthma
- Tiotropium:
  - Long acting
  - Once daily
  - Slow dissociation from M3
  - Reduces COPD exacerbations
- Aclidinium:
  - Long acting, twice daily
- Umeclidinium:
  - Long acting, once daily
- Glycopyrrolate:
  - Inhaled form used in COPD

D. Urinary antimuscarinics
- Oxybutynin:
  - Some M3 selectivity
  - Used for overactive bladder, urge incontinence, postoperative bladder spasm
  - Oral, extended-release, transdermal, intravesical forms
- Tolterodine:
  - M2/M3 selective
  - Used for urinary incontinence
- Darifenacin, solifenacin:
  - More M3 selective
  - Once daily due to long half-life
- Fesoterodine:
  - M3 selective prodrug
- Trospium:
  - Quaternary amine
  - Less CNS penetration

E. GI antimuscarinics
- Dicyclomine:
  - Used for irritable bowel syndrome and intestinal spasm
- Hyoscyamine:
  - Longer acting GI antispasmodic
- Atropine + diphenoxylate:
  - Used for diarrhea
  - Atropine discourages opioid abuse

F. CNS antimuscarinics
- Benztropine, trihexyphenidyl:
  - Parkinson disease tremor
  - Drug-induced extrapyramidal symptoms

G. Cholinergic poisoning drugs
- Atropine:
  - Treats muscarinic effects of organophosphate poisoning
  - Does not reverse nicotinic skeletal muscle weakness
- Pralidoxime:
  - Reactivates acetylcholinesterase before aging
  - Most useful at neuromuscular junction
  - Does not enter CNS
  - Not useful for carbamate poisoning

H. Ganglion blockers
- Mecamylamine:
  - Blocks neuronal nicotinic receptors
  - Enters CNS
  - Rarely used
- Hexamethonium:
  - Historical antihypertensive
  - Blocks autonomic ganglia
- Trimethaphan:
  - Short acting; mostly obsolete

5. Clinical Correlations

A. Overactive bladder / urge incontinence
- Mechanism: involuntary detrusor contractions
- Main receptor: M3
- Treatment:
  - Oxybutynin
  - Tolterodine
  - Darifenacin
  - Solifenacin
  - Fesoterodine
  - Trospium
- Adverse effects:
  - Dry mouth
  - Constipation
  - Blurred vision
  - Urinary retention
  - Confusion in elderly

B. COPD
- Vagal tone contributes to bronchoconstriction.
- Antimuscarinics block M3 receptors on airway smooth muscle.
- Tiotropium, umeclidinium, aclidinium = long-acting muscarinic antagonists.
- Often combined with long-acting beta2 agonists.

C. Eye exam
- Antimuscarinics cause:
  - Mydriasis
  - Cycloplegia
- Tropicamide is short acting and useful for funduscopic exam.
- Avoid in angle-closure glaucoma.

D. Motion sickness
- Scopolamine patch prevents vestibular-induced nausea/vomiting.
- Common adverse effects: sedation, dry mouth, blurred vision.

E. Organophosphate poisoning
- Findings:
  - Muscarinic: salivation, lacrimation, urination, diarrhea, bronchorrhea, bronchospasm, bradycardia, miosis
  - Nicotinic: fasciculations, weakness, paralysis
  - CNS: confusion, seizures, coma
- Treatment:
  - ABCs and decontamination
  - Atropine for muscarinic symptoms
  - Pralidoxime before aging to restore acetylcholinesterase
  - Benzodiazepines for seizures

6. Adverse Effects / Toxicity / Pitfalls

Classic antimuscarinic toxicity:
- Dry mouth
- Mydriasis
- Cycloplegia
- Blurred vision
- Tachycardia
- Constipation
- Urinary retention
- Hot, flushed skin
- Fever
- Agitation, delirium, hallucinations

Mnemonic:
- Dry as a bone = decreased secretions
- Blind as a bat = mydriasis/cycloplegia
- Red as a beet = flushing
- Mad as a hatter = delirium
- Hot as a hare = hyperthermia

Contraindications / cautions:
- Angle-closure glaucoma
- BPH or urinary retention
- Elderly patients with cognitive impairment
- Gastric retention
- Children: high risk of atropine fever

Important pitfall:
- Atropine reverses muscarinic effects of organophosphates but does not reverse nicotinic paralysis.
- Pralidoxime must be given before aging of the organophosphate-enzyme complex.
- Physostigmine can reverse severe antimuscarinic toxicity but is used cautiously due to seizure and cardiac risk.

7. Important Comparisons

Antimuscarinic vs Cholinomimetic Effects

| Organ/System | Cholinomimetic Effect | Antimuscarinic Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Eye | Miosis, accommodation | Mydriasis, cycloplegia |
| Heart | Bradycardia | Tachycardia |
| Bronchi | Bronchoconstriction, secretions | Bronchodilation, decreased secretions |
| GI tract | Increased motility/secretions | Decreased motility/secretions |
| Bladder | Detrusor contraction | Detrusor relaxation |
| Sweat glands | Sweating | Decreased sweating, hyperthermia |
| CNS | Cholinergic activation | Sedation or delirium at high dose |

Tertiary vs Quaternary Antimuscarinics

| Feature | Tertiary Amines | Quaternary Amines |
|---|---|---|
| CNS entry | Good | Poor |
| Absorption | Better | Poor oral absorption |
| Examples | Atropine, scopolamine, oxybutynin, tolterodine | Ipratropium, tiotropium, glycopyrrolate, trospium |
| Main issue | CNS toxicity | Peripheral effects |
| Clinical use | CNS, eye, urinary, poisoning | COPD, reduced secretions, bladder with less CNS effect |

Ophthalmic Drugs Duration

| Drug | Duration |
|---|---|
| Atropine | 5-6 days |
| Scopolamine | 3-7 days |
| Homatropine | 12-24 hours |
| Cyclopentolate | 3-6 hours |
| Tropicamide | 15-60 minutes |

Overactive Bladder Drugs

| Drug | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| Oxybutynin | M3 preference; oral/patch/intravesical |
| Tolterodine | M2/M3 selective |
| Darifenacin | More M3 selective |
| Solifenacin | More M3 selective |
| Fesoterodine | M3-selective prodrug |
| Trospium | Quaternary; less CNS entry |

Organophosphate Treatment

| Drug | Role | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Atropine | Blocks muscarinic excess | No effect on nicotinic paralysis |
| Pralidoxime | Regenerates acetylcholinesterase | Must be before aging; poor CNS entry |
| Benzodiazepines | Treat seizures | Supportive |
| Decontamination | Prevents further absorption | Must protect healthcare workers |

8. Must-Know Exam Points

- Atropine is a competitive reversible muscarinic antagonist.
- Atropine is nonselective for M1-M5.
- M3 mediates gland secretion, bronchoconstriction, GI contraction, bladder contraction, and miosis.
- M2 mediates bradycardia and AV nodal slowing.
- Antimuscarinics cause mydriasis and cycloplegia.
- Antimuscarinics can precipitate acute angle-closure glaucoma.
- Scopolamine is best known for motion sickness.
- Ipratropium and tiotropium are inhaled antimuscarinics for COPD/asthma.
- Tiotropium is long acting due to slow dissociation from M3 receptors.
- Oxybutynin and tolterodine treat overactive bladder/urge incontinence.
- Antimuscarinics can cause urinary retention, especially in BPH.
- Atropine treats muscarinic toxicity from organophosphate poisoning.
- Pralidoxime regenerates acetylcholinesterase before aging.
- Pralidoxime does not cross the blood-brain barrier well.
- Ganglion blockers cause orthostatic hypotension, constipation, urinary retention, and sexual dysfunction.
- Mecamylamine enters the CNS; hexamethonium is historical.

9. Case-Based Integration

Case: 63-year-old man post-prostatectomy with urinary urgency and frequency

Most likely diagnosis:
- Urge incontinence due to detrusor muscle instability after relief of chronic bladder outlet obstruction from BPH.

Why it happens:
- Long-standing obstruction causes detrusor hypertrophy and instability.
- After prostatectomy, outlet obstruction improves, but detrusor overactivity may persist.

Information to gather:
- Urinalysis and urine culture to exclude UTI
- Post-void residual urine to exclude retention/overflow
- Ask about weak stream, incomplete emptying, dysuria, hematuria, fever, nocturia
- Consider urodynamic studies if diagnosis is unclear

Treatment:
- Reassure that urgency/frequency may improve over time.
- Start antimuscarinic therapy:
  - Tolterodine ER 4 mg daily, or
  - Oxybutynin ER 5-10 mg daily, or
  - Oxybutynin transdermal patch 3.9 mg/day
- Monitor for:
  - Dry mouth
  - Constipation
  - Blurred vision
  - Confusion
  - Urinary retention
- Consider alternatives if refractory:
  - More M3-selective agents such as darifenacin/solifenacin
  - Intrabladder botulinum toxin A for refractory cases

Exam clue:
- Post-prostatectomy urgency/frequency = detrusor instability -> treat with M3-blocking antimuscarinic.

A: Atropine.

A: M2.

Q: Which muscarinic receptor mediates bladder detrusor contraction?
A: M3.

Q: Which antimuscarinic is used for motion sickness?
A: Scopolamine.

Q: Which antimuscarinic has the shortest ophthalmic duration?
A: Tropicamide.

Q: Which antimuscarinics are used for COPD?

A: Atropine.

12. Mini MCQs

1. A patient develops dry mouth, mydriasis, tachycardia, urinary retention, and delirium after ingesting jimson weed. What receptor is blocked?
Answer: Muscarinic acetylcholine receptor.

2. A man with COPD is started on a once-daily inhaled antimuscarinic that dissociates slowly from M3 receptors. What drug is most likely?
Answer: Tiotropium.

3. A patient with organophosphate poisoning receives atropine but still has muscle weakness. What drug should be added early?
Answer: Pralidoxime.

4. A patient needs a short-acting drug for funduscopic examination. Which antimuscarinic is preferred?
Answer: Tropicamide.

5. A post-prostatectomy patient has urinary urgency and frequency due to detrusor instability. What drug class is useful?
Answer: Antimuscarinic drugs, such as oxybutynin or tolterodine.
